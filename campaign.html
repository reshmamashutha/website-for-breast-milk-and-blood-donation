Breast-Milk Donation

The first moment a woman holds her kid in her arms, she experiences an emotion that is unmatched by anything else. Mother's milk provides a baby with total nutrients and acts as a barrier against numerous ailments. The little fledgling of a baby might occasionally go without this elixir. Perhaps the woman is too weak to feed the infant or the baby is delivered prematurely with insufficient strength, or there may be other medical reasons why the mother is unable to immediately nurse the child. Such circumstances can jeopardise the infant's health and immunity.
To combat such situations, the babies need a temporary alternate supply of milk.

Because the mother of a premature infant frequently cannot breastfeed for a variety of reasons.
Furthermore, sepsis and the gastrointestinal condition "Necrotizing enterocolitis" can affect premature newborns.
Yet studies have shown that giving these infants breast milk rather than formula can avert 80% of the issues in the NICU.
Here's how you can help: as a mother, you can give extra or surplus amounts of breast milk to a MILK BANK.

We are all aware that breast milk is valuable. It is regarded as "Drops of Gold" for a newborn.
Also, a breastfeeding woman who has extra or extra-large amounts of milk can donate just 1 ounce to a "Milk Bank" as a pure, unselfish act that will benefit infants in NICUs.

Who can donate milk?

Breast milk can be donated after a suitable examination and approval by a doctor to a nursing mother who is in good health,
tests negative for HIV and VDRL, is not using any medications, and is free from any evidence of Hepatitis.

Blood Donation

In order to effectively treat patients with blood and bone marrow disorders, inherited disorders of haemoglobin, and immune deficiency conditions, as well as those undergoing advanced medical and surgical procedures, blood and blood products are crucial resources. These patients include women suffering from bleeding associated with pregnancy and childbirth, children with severe anaemia caused by malaria and malnutrition, patients with blood and bone marrow disorders, patients with inherited disorders of haemoglobin, and patients with immune deficiency conditions. Blood is always needed, but not everyone who needs it has access to it. Very severe blood shortages exist in low- and middle-income nations.

In order to guarantee that everyone who requires a transfusion has access to safe blood, volunteer, unpaid blood donors who consistently donate are needed in all nations. In order to meet the demand for blood transfusions during peacetime as well as during emergencies or disasters, when there is a spike in demand for blood or when the regular operation of blood services is affected, an effective blood donor programme, characterised by widespread and active participation of the population, is essential. While the creation of an efficient blood donor programme is facilitated by a supportive social and cultural climate with a strong sense of solidarity, it is also commonly accepted that giving blood fosters social connections and helps a community come together.

Who can donate Blood?

Most people can give blood if they are in good health. There are some basic requirements one need to fulfill in order to become a blood donor.
Below are some basic eligibility guidelines Suggested by WHO:

  • Age:You are aged between 18 and 65.
    Weight:You weigh at least 50 kg.
    Health:You must be in good health at the time you donate.
    You cannot donate if you have a cold, flu, sore throat, cold sore, stomach bug or any other infection.
    If you have recently had a tattoo or body piercing you cannot donate for 6 months from the date of the procedure.
    If the body piercing was performed by a registered health professional and any inflammation has settled completely, you can donate blood after 12 hours.
    If you have visited the dentist for a minor procedure you must wait 24 hours before donating; for major work wait a month.
    You must not donate blood If you do not meet the minimum haemoglobin level for blood donation.
    Behaviours:You must not give blood:If you engaged in “at risk” sexual activity in the past 12 months
    Individuals with behaviours below will be deferred permanently:
    Have ever had a positive test for HIV (AIDS virus) Have ever injected recreational drugs.
